Ada Lovelace

The First Computer Programmer

The Remarkable Life of Ada Lovelace

1815

Born Augusta Ada Byron on December 10 in London, England. She was the only legitimate child of the poet Lord Byron and Anne Isabella Milbanke.

1816

Ada's mother separated from Lord Byron just weeks after her birth. Ada never met her father, who died when she was eight years old.

1833

At the age of 17, Ada met Charles Babbage at a party and became fascinated with his Difference Engine, a mechanical calculator.

1835

Ada married William King, who later became the Earl of Lovelace. They had three children together.

1842-1843

Ada translated an article on Babbage's Analytical Engine by Italian mathematician Luigi Menabrea. She added her own extensive notes, which contained what is considered to be the first computer program—an algorithm designed to be processed by a machine.

1843

In her notes, Ada described how the Analytical Engine could be programmed to calculate Bernoulli numbers. This is considered the first published algorithm specifically tailored for implementation on a computer.

1852

Ada Lovelace died of uterine cancer on November 27 at the age of 36, the same age at which her father had died.

Legacy

Ada's contributions to computer science were not fully appreciated until the 1950s. Today, she is recognized as a pioneer in the field. The programming language Ada was named in her honor in 1980.

"The Analytical Engine has no pretensions whatever to originate anything. It can do whatever we know how to order it to perform. It can follow analysis; but it has no power of anticipating any analytical relations or truths."

— Ada Lovelace

Ada's Contributions to Computing

Ada Lovelace's notes on the Analytical Engine include:

  • The first algorithm designed for processing by a machine
  • A vision for the capability of computers to go beyond mere calculating or number-crunching
  • The concept that computers could manipulate symbols and even create music
  • A method for the engine to repeat certain instructions, a process known today as looping
